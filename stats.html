    What is obesity?

    The CDC defines obesity as the state of being heavier than what is considered healthy. Obesity can be screened using the Body Mass Index (or BMI) measurement.


    What is the obesity epidemic?

    The obesity epidemic is the dramatic rise in obesity rates across all sectors of the population in the past ten years. This includes various ethnic groups as well as all-ages of the population. Currently, more than 1 in 3 adults and 1 in 5 children in the united states are considered obese.


    What are the causes of obesity?

    The prime causes of obesity are poor diet and lack of physical activity. The NHS outlines that poor diet constitutes eating more than the recommended amount of calories per day and involves habits such as:

    • Eating large amounts of processed or fast food
    • Eating out a lot
    • Eating larger portions than you need
    • Drinking too many sugary drinks
    • Comfort eating
    A lack of physical activity results when individuals fail to meet the recommended amount of exercise per week, which, according to the American Heart Association, should be between 75 and 150 minutes per week.


    What are the effects of obesity?

    The Centers for Disease Control outlines that obesity can cause:

    • Death
    • High blood pressure (Hypertension)
    • High LDL cholesterol, low HDL cholesterol, or high levels of triglycerides (Dyslipidemia)
    • Type 2 diabetes
    • Coronary heart disease
    • Stroke
    • Gallbladder disease
    • Osteoarthritis (a breakdown of cartilage and bone within a joint)
    • Sleep apnea and breathing problems
    • Some cancers (endometrial, breast, colon, kidney, gallbladder, and liver)
    • Low quality of life
    • Mental illness such as clinical depression, anxiety, and other mental disorders
    • Body pain and difficulty with physical functioning

    What is heart disease?

    Coronary heart disease, also known as just heart disease or coronary artery disease, is the main form of heart disease. According to the National Institutes of Health, it is a disorder that can cause heart attacks when arteries become blocked. This is a lifelong condition that is currently the number one cause of death in the United States.


    What causes heart disease?

    As outlined by the CDC, heart disease is caused by the buildup of plaque on the inside of arteries. Plaque is made up of deposits of cholesterol and other substances in the artery and can cause arteries to narrow. Risk factors for heart disease include:

    • Obesity
    • Insulin resistance or diabetes
    • High cholesterol and blood pressure
    • Family history of heart disease
    • Being physically inactive
    • Smoking
    • Eating an unhealthy diet
    • Clinical depression


    Is obesity linked to heart disease?

    Yes. An American College of Cardiology study found that for every point increase in BMI, a person is 4 percent more likely to develop heart disease. In addition to heart disease, obesity is also linked to other cardiovascular diseases such as high blood pressure, diabetes, and abnormal blood cholesterol.


    What are the effects of heart disease?

    Heart disease is known to have many adverse effects on the body. These include, but are not limited to:

    • Heart failure
    • Heart attack
    • Stroke
    • Pulmonary embolism
    • Cardiac arrest
    • Peripheral artery disease
    • Atrial Fibrillation
    • Angina

    What is health insurance?

    Health insurance is a type of insurance that helps cover the costs of medical procedures and treatments, such as yearly checkups and surgeries. Generally speaking, there exist two primary types of health insurance: public and private. Public insurance, such as Medicaid and Medicare in the United States, varies from state to state and is provided by the government. Private insurance, however, is provided by various companies that consumers can choose from.
